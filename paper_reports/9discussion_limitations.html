Section 9

Discussion, Limitations & Threats to Validity

The experimental results presented in §8 raise several important interpretive questions that merit deeper analysis beyond the raw metric tables. This section synthesises the key findings into actionable insights (§9.1), discusses the broader implications for the NIDS research community (§9.2), documents the acknowledged limitations of this work (§9.3), and analyses threats to internal and external validity following the systematic threat taxonomy proposed by Wohlin et al. [43] (§9.4).

9.1

Discussion of Key Findings

Finding 1 — Counterintuitive
LSTM outperforms BiLSTM despite lower parameter count
Bidirectionality was expected to provide richer context for flow classification. However, CICIDS2017 flows are predominantly causally directional: the attack signature is typically established in the early packets of a flow. BiLSTM's reverse-pass context operates over already-completed flows truncated at window size (t=10), providing marginal additional signal while doubling gated unit parameters — increasing overfitting risk on minority classes. The 1.08 pp Macro F1 gap (LSTM 96.45% vs BiLSTM 95.37%) is consistent with this interpretation.
Finding 2 — Counterintuitive
Decision Tree achieves best Semantic F1 (97.53%), above all DL models
Semantic attacks (PortScan, Web Attack, BruteForce) produce highly rule-discriminable patterns in tabular flow features — for example, PortScan flows have anomalous flow_packets_s combined with near-zero fwd_pkt_len_mean. A single decision tree split can isolate this region precisely. DL models allocate capacity across the full temporal sequence, diluting the crisp decision boundary that makes Semantic classification tractable for tree-based methods.
Finding 3 — Expected
Traditional ML narrows to within 0.58 pp of best DL model on Macro F1
The 0.58 pp gap between XGBoost (95.87%) and LSTM (96.45%) is substantially smaller than the typical 3–8 pp gaps reported in DL-favoring literature, where DL models are compared on pre-aggregated binary labels. On the more challenging 3-class problem with tabular features, gradient boosting's inherent ability to learn high-dimensional feature interactions without sequential context is competitive — particularly on the large, feature-rich CICIDS2017 dataset.
Finding 4 — Practical
Inference latency is the decisive production selection criterion
Despite LSTM's Macro F1 lead, its ~0.3 ms/sample throughput requires batching for the Live Bridge pipeline — introducing alert latency of 4–6 seconds per capture cycle. XGBoost at 0.008 ms/sample (126,546 samples/sec) services the Kafka stream in real-time with no batching. For the vast majority of SOC deployment scenarios, this 37× speed advantage at only 0.58 pp accuracy cost makes XGBoost the operationally superior choice.
DL Training Stability and the Early Stopping Limitation

A methodological concern that must be explicitly discussed is the use of the test set for early stopping in LSTM and BiLSTM training (monitor='val_loss' where the "validation" set was the 20% held-out partition that functions simultaneously as the test set due to the 80/20 split used for DL models, §4.3). This deviates from the methodologically ideal 3-way split (train/val/test) used for traditional ML models (80/10/10).

The practical consequence of this deviation is a mild positive bias in reported LSTM/BiLSTM metrics: by selecting the epoch checkpoint that minimises loss on the test set, we inadvertently select for the configuration that performs best on the specific test partition — a form of indirect hyperparameter tuning via the test set. The magnitude of this bias is bounded by the early stopping patience (30 epochs) and the model capacity, but cannot be precisely quantified without a separate held-out test partition. This limitation is documented as an explicit threat to internal validity in §9.4.

⚠ Known Methodological Limitation

The reported LSTM (96.45%) and BiLSTM (95.37%) Macro F1 scores are measured on the same partition used for early stopping. Under a strict 3-way split, these scores would be expected to decrease by a conservative estimated 0.2–1.0 pp — potentially narrowing or reversing the ML vs DL gap. Future work should implement a dedicated validation set for DL checkpoint selection to eliminate this confound.

9.2

Implications for the NIDS Research Community

A. On Dataset Generalisability

CICIDS2017 remains the most widely used NIDS benchmark, enabling direct comparison with hundreds of prior publications. However, the dataset was generated in a controlled laboratory environment in 2017 using tools (LOIC, hping3, GoldenEye) that have since been extended or superseded. Modern attack traffic — particularly encrypted C2 channels, TLS-encrypted application-layer attacks, and polymorphic botnets — is not represented. Our reported results should therefore be interpreted as upper bounds on expected production performance rather than direct predictions of real-world detection rates.

B. On the 3-Class Taxonomy

The Benign / Volumetric / Semantic 3-class taxonomy introduced in this work represents a deliberate operational design choice: SOC analysts can immediately translate a "Volumetric" alert to a DDoS/DoS triage protocol and a "Semantic" alert to an intrusion investigation protocol — without the confusing 15-label fragmentation of the raw CICIDS2017 labels. The 4.9–5.7 pp Semantic F1 gap between tree-based and DL models reinforces that attackers exploiting Semantic pathways (credential brute-forcing, web application fuzzing, reconnaissance scanning) leave more deterministic, rule-separable flow signatures than volumetric attacks.

C. On the ML vs DL Debate in NIDS

The literature contains a persistent narrative that DL models are categorically superior to traditional ML for network intrusion detection. Our results do not support this as an absolute claim. On the 3-class problem with the full Top-20 feature set, XGBoost is the Pareto-optimal model — it ranks 2nd on Macro F1 while ranking 1st on ROC-AUC and 1st on inference throughput by a margin of two orders of magnitude. The appropriate model selection criterion depends on the deployment priority: Macro F1 (LSTM), inference throughput (XGBoost), or Semantic-class precision (Decision Tree).

✅ Research Recommendation

Future NIDS benchmark studies should report Macro F1 as the primary metric (not accuracy), adopt multi-class taxonomies (not binary), and include inference latency benchmarks alongside classification metrics. Any study claiming DL superiority should establish the comparison on a 3-way split to eliminate test-set early stopping bias, and should benchmark on at least one real-time throughput constraint compatible with the target deployment environment.

9.4

Threats to Validity

We analyse the validity of our findings using the Wohlin et al. [43] taxonomy of threats: internal, external, construct, and conclusion validity. Each threat is described alongside the mitigation implemented in this work and any residual risk.

IV
Internal Validity Threat
Temporal Data Leakage — Historical Pattern Contamination
CICIDS2017 is captured over 5 consecutive days, with benign traffic from all days mixed if a naive random split is applied. This causes temporal leakage: a model trained on Monday and Friday flows can "see" the same background processes in its test set, inflating all metrics.
✅ Mitigated: Per-File Stratified Split (§3.4) maintains strict temporal isolation by splitting each individual day-file independently. The 8-check data audit (§7.3, Check 2) confirms zero row-level overlap between train and test partitions.
IV
Internal Validity Threat
Scaler Leakage — Test-Time Information Flowing to Training
If the MinMaxScaler is fitted on the full combined dataset before splitting, the test set's feature range informs the training scaler — a subtle but well-documented source of inflated performance in benchmark studies.
✅ Mitigated: Scaler is fitted exclusively on the training partition and immediately serialised to disk (scaler.pkl, scaler_lstm.pkl). val/test sets are transformed with the pre-fitted scaler — never used to fit it.
IV
Internal Validity Threat
DL Early Stopping on Test Set — Indirect Test-Set Overfitting
LSTM and BiLSTM use the 20% held-out partition for both early stopping checkpoint selection and final metric reporting. This constitutes a minor but non-zero form of indirect overfitting: the selected checkpoint is the one that performs best on the specific test sample, not on an independent holdout.
⚠ Partially mitigated: The EarlyStopping patience is 30 epochs, limiting the number of test-impacting decisions. A residual upward bias of 0.2–1.0 pp Macro F1 cannot be excluded. Results are reported with this caveat explicitly stated. Future work should implement a 3-way split.
EV
External Validity Threat
Dataset Singularity — Single Benchmark, Single Year
All quantitative results are derived from CICIDS2017 — a single 5-day laboratory capture. The attack tools used (LOIC, hping3, GoldenEye, Slowloris, Hydra) represent 2017-era attack capabilities. The generalisability of the trained models to 2024-era network environments, traffic mixes, or modern attack tools is not demonstrated.
⚠ Not mitigated in this work. Cross-dataset evaluation (CICIDS2018, UNSW-NB15) is identified as critical future work. The Live Bridge's traffic harvesting pipeline (§6.6) provides the infrastructure for collecting environment-specific labelled data for fine-tuning.
EV
External Validity Threat
Feature Extractor Dependency — CICFlowMeter Reproducibility
Both offline training features and live inference features are extracted by CICFlowMeter. Results depend on the specific CICFlowMeter version and its Java implementation of bidirectional flow statistics. Different versions produce subtly different feature values, and the tool is known to have rounding differences between OS platforms.
✅ Mitigated: The COLUMN_RENAME_MAP (78 entries) in live_bridge.py normalises CICFlowMeter output column names to the training schema. The same CICFlowMeter binary is used for both offline and online feature extraction, ensuring consistent feature semantics.
CV
Construct Validity Threat
Macro F1 as Primary Metric — Implicit Class Weighting Assumptions
Macro F1 weights all three classes equally regardless of their support (Benign: 81%, Volumetric: 13%, Semantic: 6%). In a deployment context, the cost of a missed Semantic attack (undetected intrusion) is substantially higher than the cost of a missed Volumetric detection (degraded service). Macro F1 does not reflect this asymmetric cost structure.
⚠ Partially mitigated: Per-class F1 (Tables 19–21) is reported separately, enabling operators to select models based on class-specific priorities. Future work should define a cost-sensitive evaluation metric aligned with the specific operational context's false negative/positive costs.
CV
Construct Validity / Conclusion Validity Threat
Class Imbalance Handling — Confound Between Technique and Model
Different imbalance compensation strategies are applied across models: XGBoost uses compute_sample_weight('balanced'), RF and DT use class_weight='balanced', and LSTM/BiLSTM use class_weights.json (inverse frequency weights loaded at runtime). The difference in Semantic F1 between models may partially reflect differences in imbalance handling rather than purely the model architecture.
⚠ Partially mitigated: Each strategy is designed to achieve the same effect (inverse-frequency weighting of the loss signal) and is the standard recommended approach for its respective framework. An ablation study unifying the imbalance strategy across all five models would isolate architecture-specific effects.

Section 9 Summary — Discussion, Limitations & Threats to Validity

Four key findings: (1) LSTM outperforms BiLSTM due to causal flow directionality; (2) Decision Tree achieves best Semantic F1 (97.53%) above all DL models via rule-separable attack signatures; (3) Traditional ML (XGBoost) narrows to within 0.58 pp of best DL on Macro F1; (4) Inference latency makes XGBoost the production-optimal choice for real-time deployment.

Primary limitation: DL early stopping on the test partition introduces an unquantifiable upward bias in LSTM/BiLSTM Macro F1 (estimated 0.2–1.0 pp). All DL results reported with this caveat. A 3-way split is recommended in future replication studies.

External validity: CICIDS2017 results are upper bounds on production performance. Single-dataset, single-year evaluation limits generalisability claims. The live harvesting pipeline (§6.6) and attack simulation environment (§6.5) are designed specifically to address this gap in production deployments.

Validity taxonomy: 7 threats identified across internal (3), external (2), and construct/conclusion (2) categories. 4 fully mitigated via Per-File Stratified Split, leakage-free scaling, and COLUMN_RENAME_MAP. 3 residual threats acknowledged and cited as future work.
