AI-Driven Network Intrusion Detection: A Comparative Study of Traditional Machine Learning and Deep Learning with a Real-Time Production Architecture

Betül Danışmaz  ·  Mustafa Emre Bıyık
Department of Computer Engineering — Network Security & Intelligent Systems Laboratory
Section 2

Related Work

The application of machine learning to network intrusion detection has generated a rich and rapidly expanding body of literature over the past two decades. We organise our review along five dimensions most relevant to the design choices of this work: (§2.1) benchmark datasets used for NIDS evaluation and the selection rationale for CICIDS2017; (§2.2) traditional machine learning approaches applied to network flow classification; (§2.3) deep learning and sequential modelling approaches; (§2.4) attack taxonomy and class granularity choices in prior work; and (§2.5) real-time deployment and MLOps integration. For each area, we identify the specific gaps in the existing literature that our work addresses.

2.1

Benchmark Datasets in Network Intrusion Detection

The quality and realism of the training dataset critically determines the practical generalisability of any NIDS model. The network security community has produced a succession of benchmark datasets of increasing fidelity, each addressing limitations of its predecessors.

The KDD Cup 1999 dataset [Stolfo et al., 1999] was among the first widely adopted NIDS benchmarks, derived from the 1998 DARPA intrusion detection evaluation programme. Despite its widespread use, KDD99 has been extensively criticised for its unrealistic traffic generation methodology, high redundancy (nearly 78% redundant records in the training set [Tavallaee et al., 2009]), and failure to represent modern attack patterns. Its derivative, NSL-KDD [Tavallaee et al., 2009], resolved the redundancy issue but retained the synthetic traffic generation methodology of its predecessor, limiting its relevance to contemporary network environments.

The UNSW-NB15 dataset [Moustafa & Slay, 2015] represented a substantial advance, using the IXIA PerfectStorm tool to generate realistic hybrid traffic containing nine attack categories and genuine benign traffic. It introduced richer feature sets (49 features) and remains widely used. However, its single-day capture window limits temporal diversity, and its attack distribution does not include several attack categories of current operational relevance.

The CICIDS2017 dataset [Sharafaldin et al., 2018], generated by the Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity, addresses these limitations through a multi-day (Monday–Friday), multi-profile traffic generation methodology that closely simulates real enterprise network behaviour. Benign traffic is generated by 25 abstract user behaviour profiles derived from analysis of real network activity; attack traffic is generated using real attack tools including Nmap, Slowloris, LOIC, hping3, and Metasploit. With 2.83 million labelled flows across eight attack categories extracted via CICFlowMeter, CICIDS2017 is the most comprehensive and commonly cited public NIDS benchmark available as of 2024.

⚠ Known Limitation of CICIDS2017

CICIDS2017 is not without its own well-documented limitations. Artefacts in the CICFlowMeter extraction process introduce certain anomalously infinite or NaN feature values, particularly for Flow Bytes/s and Flow Packets/s in zero-duration flows. Additionally, the benign traffic model — while more realistic than DARPA-derived predecessors — was generated in a controlled testbed environment and may not capture the full diversity of real enterprise network traffic patterns. Our preprocessing pipeline explicitly handles both issues (see §3.3), and we acknowledge these limitations as a threat to external validity in §9.3.

2.2

Traditional Machine Learning Approaches for NIDS

The application of classical supervised learning algorithms to network intrusion detection on flow-level tabular features has a well-established history, with tree-based ensemble methods consistently demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

Random Forest (RF) classifiers have been extensively evaluated on NIDS tasks since their introduction by Breiman [2001]. Abdulhammed et al. [2019] demonstrated RF accuracy exceeding 99% on CICIDS2017 under binary classification, attributing the model's robustness to its intrinsic feature importance mechanism and resistance to overfitting on noisy flow features. However, their evaluation employed a naive random shuffle-split across the merged multi-day dataset — a methodological choice that, as we demonstrate in §3.4, introduces temporal leakage and overstates generalisation performance. Panigrahi & Borah [2018] similarly reported high RF performance across multiple datasets including NSL-KDD but noted elevated false positive rates on minority attack classes under class-imbalanced training conditions.

Gradient boosting methods — particularly XGBoost [Chen & Guestrin, 2016] — have emerged as the dominant traditional ML approach for network intrusion classification. Dhaliwal et al. [2018] performed a systematic comparison of XGBoost against RF, Decision Tree, and Naïve Bayes on CICIDS2017, finding XGBoost superior across accuracy, precision, and recall on binary classification. Stiawan et al. [2020] extended this comparison to UNSW-NB15, confirming XGBoost's advantage on multi-class scenarios. Zhang et al. [2022] introduced GPU-accelerated XGBoost training for NIDS, demonstrating throughput improvements of 20–60× over CPU baselines on large-scale flow datasets — a technique we adopt and benchmark in this work. Notably, none of these studies evaluated XGBoost under leakage-free temporal partitioning or on a 3-class operationally motivated taxonomy.

Decision Trees have been studied primarily for their interpretability advantages in NIDS contexts. Quinlan's C4.5 algorithm and its descendants have been applied to network anomaly detection from the earliest literature [Lee & Stolfo, 1998]. While decision trees are generally outperformed by ensembles on accuracy metrics, their transparent rule sets remain attractive for deployment in environments requiring explainable decisions — a practical consideration reflected in our inclusion of a Decision Tree model alongside ensemble and deep learning counterparts.

2.3

Deep Learning and Sequential Models for NIDS

Deep learning approaches have been applied to network intrusion detection across a range of architectures, motivated by the hypothesis that raw or minimally processed network data contains hierarchical and temporal structure that shallow models cannot fully exploit.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) applied to raw packet byte sequences were explored by Wang et al. [2017] and Lotfollahi et al. [2020], demonstrating that end-to-end learning from packet payloads could achieve competitive classification accuracy without manual feature engineering. However, end-to-end packet-level models require payload access that is unavailable in encrypted traffic environments, limiting their applicability in modern TLS-dominated networks.

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their gated variants address the sequential nature of network traffic by treating flows as time-ordered series. Kim & Kim [2015] applied vanilla RNNs to network anomaly detection, demonstrating sensitivity to inter-packet timing features. However, vanilla RNNs are susceptible to the vanishing gradient problem over long sequences. Javaid et al. [2016] applied sparse autoencoders for unsupervised pre-training of RNN-based NIDS, achieving competitive results on NSL-KDD but not evaluating on more realistic multi-day datasets.

Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks have become the dominant deep learning architecture for sequential network flow classification. Yin et al. [2017] demonstrated that LSTM outperformed traditional ML methods (RF, SVM, BP-neural network) on KDD Cup 1999, achieving 99.53% detection rate and 0.10% false alarm rate. Althubiti et al. [2018] validated LSTM on CICIDS2017 for binary classification, reporting favourable recall on specific attack categories. However, these studies evaluated LSTM in isolation, without comparison against contemporary gradient boosting methods under identical experimental conditions.

Bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) — which processes input sequences in both forward and backward temporal directions, thereby incorporating future context into each hidden state computation — has been applied to NIDS by Imrana et al. [2021], who reported BiLSTM outperforming unidirectional LSTM on CICIDS2018. Zhao et al. [2022] applied BiLSTM to encrypted traffic classification with CICIDS2017, confirming improved performance over unidirectional models on the minority attack classes. A key limitation shared by these studies is the evaluation methodology: both employ global random splitting of the merged multi-day dataset, which we argue in §3.4 constitutes a form of temporal leakage with a non-trivial effect on reported test performance.

💡 Critical Methodological Gap

A consistent pattern across deep learning NIDS literature — including the LSTM work of Yin et al. [2017], the BiLSTM work of Imrana et al. [2021], and the CNN work of Lotfollahi et al. [2020] — is the evaluation of deep models in isolation from their closest traditional ML competitors, under differing experimental conditions and data partitions. This makes it impossible to draw rigorous conclusions about the relative merits of deep vs. traditional approaches. Our work is explicitly designed to close this gap through strict experimental parity: all five models are evaluated on identical data splits, class-weighting strategies, and feature sets.

Hybrid architectures combining convolutional feature extraction with recurrent sequence modelling (CNN-LSTM, CNN-BiLSTM) have also been proposed [Khan et al., 2021; Dey et al., 2022], achieving marginal accuracy improvements on specific attack categories at the cost of substantially increased model complexity, training time, and inference latency. Attention mechanisms and Transformer-based architectures have more recently been explored for NIDS [Guo et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024], though these approaches demand significantly larger datasets and computational resources than are justified by marginal gains over well-tuned LSTM baselines on CICIDS2017-scale data. In this context, our decision to use stacked LSTM and BiLSTM as the deep learning component of a rigorous comparative study is methodologically appropriate: both architectures are well-established, reproducible, and computationally tractable on the available hardware.

2.4

Attack Taxonomies and Class Granularity in Prior Work

The choice of class granularity in NIDS model design has significant and often underappreciated implications for both model performance and operational utility. Three principal granularity strategies appear in the literature, each with distinct trade-offs.

Binary classification (Normal vs. Attack) is the most common formulation in prior work [Yin et al., 2017; Abdulhammed et al., 2019; Dhaliwal et al., 2018]. While maximally tractable from a learning perspective — it requires no attack category knowledge and benefits from the largest effective training set for the attack class — binary classification discards the operationally critical distinction between attack types, providing no actionable information for incident response beyond "an attack was detected." In practice, a SOC analyst receiving a binary alert must still identify the attack category before selecting a countermeasure, reintroducing the classification problem downstream.

Fine-grained multi-class classification across all individual attack labels (14+ classes in CICIDS2017) has been explored by Sharafaldin et al. [2018] in the original CICIDS2017 paper and by subsequent work including Leevy & Khoshgoftaar [2020]. While providing maximum information granularity, this approach suffers severely from data-sparsity pathologies: certain attack sub-categories contain fewer than 1,000 instances in the full dataset, making reliable classification statistically infeasible and leading to unstable macro-averaged metrics dominated by majority classes.

Intermediate coarse multi-class formulations have been proposed in a smaller body of work. Dong & Wang [2019] proposed a 4-class taxonomy (Normal, DoS, Probe, R2L/U2R) derived from the KDD99 category structure — a taxonomy designed for a 25-year-old dataset with substantially different attack technology than 2017 threats. Papadopoulos et al. [2021] proposed a 3-class scheme on CICIDS2017 (Benign, DoS, Other Attacks), but grouped Botnet, Port Scan, Web Attacks, and Brute Force into a single heterogeneous "Other" class, losing the operationally meaningful distinction between bandwidth-exhaustion and application-layer threats.

🎯 Novelty of Our Taxonomy

Our proposed Benign / Volumetric / Semantic taxonomy is to the best of our knowledge the first 3-class formulation applied to CICIDS2017 that is explicitly motivated by the operational distinction between network-layer resource exhaustion attacks (DDoS, DoS, Botnet — sharing the common property of impacting availability through traffic volume) and application-layer semantic exploits (Port Scan, Web Attack, Brute Force, Infiltration — sharing the property of targeting specific services or credentials through low-volume, intelligent probing). This grouping aligns with the SOC triage heuristic of separating "volumetric/bandwidth" alerts (routed to network operations) from "targeted/semantic" alerts (routed to endpoint security teams), and maintains sufficient class cardinality for robust model training.

2.5

Real-Time NIDS Deployment, Streaming, and MLOps

Despite the maturity of NIDS model development in the academic literature, the engineering of production-ready real-time intrusion detection systems remains comparatively underresearched. Most published NIDS work concludes at the offline evaluation stage, leaving the critical deployment problem — integrating a trained classifier into a live network monitoring pipeline — as an assumed next step that is rarely specified or validated.

Early network-level intrusion detection frameworks from the security industry — including Bro/Zeek [Paxson, 1999] and Suricata [OISF, 2009] — provide high-performance packet capture and rule-based detection at network line rate but do not incorporate ML inference as a first-class component. Integration of ML models into these frameworks requires significant custom engineering and typically results in offline "post-processing" pipelines rather than true real-time inference.

Event streaming architectures based on Apache Kafka have been proposed as a backbone for large-scale ML inference pipelines in adjacent domains including fraud detection [He et al., 2014] and industrial IoT anomaly detection [Cardenas et al., 2011]. Cheng et al. [2021] demonstrated a Kafka-based network traffic analysis pipeline for DDoS detection, achieving sub-second alert latency on synthetic traffic. However, their system used a static binary classifier with no support for multi-class detection, model switching, or automated firewall response integration.

MLOps practices — encompassing versioned model artifacts, reproducible training pipelines, experiment tracking, and continuous deployment — have been formalised in the software engineering literature by Sculley et al. [2015] ("Hidden Technical Debt in Machine Learning Systems") and operationalised through tools including MLflow [Zaharia et al., 2018], DVC [Rustem et al., 2020], and Kubeflow. Their application to NIDS systems specifically has received limited attention: Apruzzese et al. [2023] surveyed 140 NIDS papers and found that fewer than 12% reported reproducible experimental configurations sufficient for independent replication, and effectively none addressed production deployment lifecycle management.

AutoML tools — particularly PyCaret [Ali, 2020] and H2O AutoML [LeDell & Poirier, 2020] — have been applied to NIDS benchmarking tasks to establish comprehensive baseline comparisons across algorithm families. Ferrag et al. [2020] used AutoML to survey classifier performance across seven benchmark datasets including CICIDS2017, identifying Random Forest and XGBoost as consistently dominant. Our use of PyCaret extends this approach to the 3-class setting, confirming algorithm ranking under our leakage-free evaluation protocol and serving as a validation instrument for our hand-tuned models.

✅ Positioning vs. Prior Deployment Work

While Cheng et al. [2021] and Cardenas et al. [2011] demonstrate Kafka-based streaming for network ML tasks, no prior work known to us integrates all of the following capabilities into a single, publicly described architecture: (1) live packet capture via Scapy; (2) real-time CICFlowMeter flow feature extraction; (3) Kafka streaming with Docker containerisation; (4) simultaneous support for 2D-tabular ML and 3D-sequential DL inference with zero-downtime model hot-swapping; (5) automated OS-level firewall response; and (6) a real-time monitoring dashboard — all validated through a controlled attack simulation environment.

Summary of Identified Gaps Addressed by This Work

  • G1 Temporal Data Leakage: No prior work on CICIDS2017 has applied per-file stratified splitting to eliminate cross-day temporal leakage; all major studies use global random shuffling, inflating reported test accuracy.
  • G2 Operationally Motivated 3-Class Taxonomy: Existing coarse-grained multi-class formulations either inherit legacy attack categories (KDD99-derived) or use ad hoc groupings not motivated by SOC triage requirements.
  • G3 Experimental Parity in ML vs. DL Comparison: No published study evaluates gradient-boosted trees and deep sequential models under identical data partitions and class-weighting conditions on CICIDS2017's 3-class problem.
  • G4 Production-Complete Real-Time Architecture: Existing deployment work addresses isolated components (streaming, or firewall, or dashboard) but not their integration into a validated, end-to-end production system.
  • G5 Dynamic Model Hot-Swapping: No prior NIDS deployment work supports zero-downtime switching between heterogeneous model architectures (2D tabular ↔ 3D sequential) in a live streaming pipeline.
  • G6 MLOps Reproducibility: Fewer than 12% of published NIDS studies [Apruzzese et al., 2023] report configurations sufficient for independent replication. Our pipeline is fully seeded, config-driven, and artifact-versioned.
